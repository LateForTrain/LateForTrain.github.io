the aircraft with the transmitter

For long the only way that people outside the an aircraft could know where the aircraft was, was either asking the pilot (over radio), see it on radar, or well spotting it in visual range.  As the airspace filled up, it became increasingly important for air traffic control (ATC) to know where all aircraft were.  In March 2003 ADS-B was demonstrated for the first time to Civil Air Patrol (CAP) and soon the technology started to appear on more and more aircraft.

So yes for the tile of this page we now understand that the aircraft has a transmitter (radio sending information) that broadcast the ADS-B information, but where does the aircraft get the information and what information is transmitted.  To start, currently it is not mandated that aircraft have ADS-B transmitter and when they do have a ADS-B transmitter it is not mandated what information they need to send.  This will however change in 2020 (for some aircraft at least), when it will be mandated to have ADS-B when flying in controlled airspaces.

As a minimum all ADS-B transitions will contain the aircraft's  ICAO address.  This address is unique and looks something like this - 4AB358.   With this information one can actually find a lot of information by doing a lookup in a public database like Airframes.org (you have to register, but it is free).

Following the unique address the are a few information categories that can also be send by the ADS-b transmitter and these are:

  1. a)  Aircraft identification
  2. b)  Surface position
  3. c)  Airborne position (w/ Baro Altitude)
  4. d)  Airborne velocities
  5. e)  Airborne position (w/ GNSS Height)
  6. f)  Aircraft status
  7. g)  Target state and status information
  8. h)  Aircraft operation status

As you can see there is quite a bit of information the can be broadcast and all of this information is supplied by the aircraft's computers and mission systems.
